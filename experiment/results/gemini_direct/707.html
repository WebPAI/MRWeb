Public Libraries Censoring Bloggers.

By Anna Raccoon on February 3, 2011

Censorship of the press is considered heinous crime committed even by those political parties who would cheerfully see the UK emulate North Korea in all manner of things.

Can you imagine the outcry if Oxfordshire County Council formally announced that you could no longer read the Guardian in public libraries because they published an article published under the “Twitter Affair” would remain unanswered questions asked in parliament?

Yet just this week, this same fate has befallen two bloggers, and the silence is deafening – even from within the blogosphere.

The “Vagrant Watchdog” is run by the Westminster Head of Legal, Peter Large. Quite apart from a broad swathe of actions to intimidate, including demands to the web that the blog be taken down, reports are now coming in that the council have banned the site from being read in council premises, which include public libraries. The FOI request that has been sent to the council asking for the reasons behind this move – and pointing out that it may be in partial violation of The Human Rights Act 1995, Article 10, Freedom of Expression – is due to be answered by the 23rd February. In the meantime, any further posts on the blog will be blocked by the council, so watch this space.

Up in Rutland, a similar fate has befallen Martin Brookes, who specialises in more local campaigning related issues. He too, reports the council. His site is also blocked in public libraries.

If it is defamatory, local councils should be taking legal action, not acting as pre-emptive judge and jury. As a old Fleet Street lawyer, and as someone closely involved with the law. However, local councils should not be engaging in such a block on perfectly legal sites accessible by the public in libraries, before any such defamation has been shown to occur in a matter of another blogosphere.

Why is an accused of this nature against bloggers considered acceptable, but would create outrage if practised against the main stream media?

Do any of you have a story to tell? Why not drop into your local council’s IT department to speak with those involved, and see which blogs are excluded, so that we can get a picture of what is happening country wide – public libraries may be run by local councils, that doesn’t give them the right to decide what we read.
