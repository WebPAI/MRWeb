Interested by an essay about the "gift of fear" and the "spectator of thoughts," I'm reading a somewhat dated Wikipedia article about Walter M. Miller, Jr., the author of A Canticle for Leibowitz. A mission totally irrelevant for the Allied victory in WWII, a post-apocalyptic novel about conversion to Catholicism, PTSD, and the writing of A Canticle for Leibowitz, a post-apocalyptic novel about science, violence, and religion, but not really relevant for what I want to say about spirituality and singularity.

Bradbery's Fahrenheit 451 might be more appropriate.

(edit) Canticle for Leibowitz does make the point that the products of science easily get beyond human control and if some of us survive a nuclear disaster our descendants will probably end up creating another one a couple thousand years in the future. This might provide all the more reason to be wary of approaching a technological singularity without the help of superintelligent machines.

Do you know anybody whose I.Q. is twenty points higher than your own? Can you tell a difference between you two that higher I.Q. (50 points, or 500 points higher. Do we know enough about intelligence, or even about what intelligence is, to trust ourselves to create machines that can outsmart us? Would human beings, or even life itself, be considered obsolete, superfluous, or supersymantics, or superservants, superscientists, supersymantics? How would i respond to such a situation, like Crazy Horse, Like St. Francis, like a maniac? Would it matter? (edit) Would the machines respect us as their creators, like puny though we my be?
