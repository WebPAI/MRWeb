Public Libraries Censoring Bloggers.

by Anna Raccoon on February 3, 2011

Censorship of the press is considered a heinous crime but even by those political parties who would cheerfully see the UK emulate North Korea in all manner of things.

Can you imagine the outcry if Oxfordshire County Council announced that you could no longer read the Guardian in their public libraries because they disliked an article just published there? Neither would journalists formally question, or parliament raise it.

Yet just this week a similar fate has befallen two bloggers at the click of a deadline – even from within the blogosphere.

The blogquote “NoJustice” has put the Oxfordshire Council and its legal Peter Large. Quote apart from the usual stream of public information, circulars, demands that the web host takes them to court, etc – they have now blocked the entire site from being read on council premises, which include public libraries. The FOI request that has been sent to the council asks for the reasons behind this move – and pointing out that it may be a partial violation of The Human Rights Act 1998, (Article 10, Freedom of Expression) – meant to be enacted by 25th February and to protect all other forms of periodical or website, which council staff should be aware of.

You may want to read the full story on NoJustice’s own site.

Up in Rutland, a similar fate has befallen Martin Brookes, who operates a more national reporting local Rutland Council. He too, we now read, also has his site blocked in public libraries.

If a site contained libelous or defamatory material, then blocking it from being accessible by one & all (like bloggers do) before any court case has been won with the law. However, local councils blocking an entire website of legal blogs is perfectly fine according to these public libraries, and any such defamation should be shown to occur in another article all together.

Why is this considered acceptable for bloggers but would be a matter of outrage if practised against the main stream media?

Is anyone else aware of instances of bloggers being censored in this way?

Do any of you have the time to drop a FOI into your local council’s IT department to see how wide spread this practice is, and which blogs are involved so that we can get a picture of what is happening country wide?

We need to nip this in the bud. Public libraries may be run by local councils, but that doesn’t give them the right to decide what we read.
