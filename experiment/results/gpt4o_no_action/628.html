Acute Hepatitis

  1. Common sx: anorexia, nausea, vomiting, fever, malaise. Maybe jaundice
  2. Dx by increased ALT/AST, often >1000 IU/L
    • If ALT/AST >10x proportionately to ALP/ALT, suspect Biliary Disease (eg: stone)
  3. Acute Pancreatitis can mimic hepatitic other causes: Lipase
    • If explains jaundice, consider pancreatic cancer (CT), hemolytic anemia (CBC)
  4. Prothrombin Time defines severity (Clx: <10, >40 severe)

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS OF ACUTE HEPATITIS

  • Hepatitis A
    • Hep A IgM antibody = Acute Hep A
    • Total Hep A Ab pos, IgM neg = already immune
  • Hepatitis B
    • Both of the following tests should be positive
      • HepB surface antigen (HBsAg)
      • HepB core IgM antibody
        • Only HBsAg Pos = Chronic Hep B
  • Hepatitis C
    • Hep C Viral Load (HCV RNA) (esp. if risk factors)
    • HepC Antibody (Anti-HCV) Pos: shows converted for a while
      • Both pos: could be acute or chronic
  • Hepatitis D
    • Only in pts w/ Chronic HepB. Hep D rare in US.
    • Hep D Antibody (rarely ordered)
  • Hepatitis E
    • If world travel, no commercial test available
    • 5% seropositivity in pregnancy (call CDC for test kit)
  • Mononucleosis
    • Epstein Barr IgM Antibody
    • Heterophile (Monospot) may take weeks to convert
  • Other Microbes
    • Self-limiting (eg. CMV, Toxo, Herpesviridae 6, 7, etc.)
    • Don't Test
  • Alcoholic Hepatitis
    • History of recent binge
    • AST > ALT, usually in 200-300 IU range
  • Toxins
    • History: Meds, Environment, Lead, Mushrooms, etc.
  • Wilson's Disease (very rare)
    • Low serum ceruloplasmin
